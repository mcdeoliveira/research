Background

The stiffness of an object can be described as its resistance to deformation due to an applied force. In other words, the stiffness quantifies how much the object stretch when a load is applied. For linear axially loaded members (such as a simple bar or string) the stiffness (<math>k</math>) is related to the change in length (<math>\delta</math>) and the applied force (<math>F</math>) as follows: <math>F = k \, \delta \quad \Longrightarrow \quad k = \frac{F}{\delta}</math> This equation is known as Hooke’s Law. You will use Hooke’s Law to experimentally find the stiffness of various configurations of elastic members.

Series Connection: Pretension

A string is said to have pretension if it is stretched past its rest length in a stable configuration before any load is applied to the structure. You can pretension our test setup by shortening the rest length of each string, while keeping the clamps and connection point fixed in space.

  1. Add pretension to your setup from the last experiment by leaving the ends of the strings clamped and untying the knot in the middle. With your partners help, stretch both strings and retie the knot further down the strings, so that it still remains in the center when released, but the strings are now stretched between the clamps.
