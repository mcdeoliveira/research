In this lab, we will first further explore the properties of the snelson prism. Then, we will look at possible methods of connecting individual prisms together to form a tensegrity plate.

Test 1: Twist angle

  1. Build a 3-bar snelson prism. Pay special attention to make the prism as regular as possible. That, is, make sure all the top and bottom strings are the same length, and that the prism is flat.

  2. Place the snelson prism on a large sheet of paper and mark the location of the three bottom nodes. Remove the snelson prism and connect the dots to form a triangle on the paper.

  3. Find the center of the triangle. Measure each edge drawn on your sheet and mark the center of these segments. Draw a line from this center point to the opposite vertex. Do this for all three sides. The three lines should all intersect. This point of intersection is the center of the polygon.

  4. Place the snelson prism back onto the piece of paper, properly aligned. Use a stick to mark a dot on the paper directly below one of the top nodes. Remove the prism. Draw a line connecting this new point to the center of the triangle.

  5. Measure the angle of rotation between the top and bottom polygons using the lines you just drew. To compute the angle, use either basic trigonometric principles or a protractor. Record this angle in the first row of the table

  6. Repeat steps (a)-(e) for snelson prisms with 4,5, and 6 bars. Record the results in the table.

Discussion

  1. Which prism had the largest twist angle? the smallest?
  2. How would you expect the twist angle of a 20-bar snelson prism to compare with the four you tested?
  3. It is known from theory that the twist angle (<math>\alpha</math>) of an <math>n</math>-bar snelson prism can be given (in radians) by the following formula: <math>\alpha = \frac{\pi}{2} - \frac{\pi}{n}</math> Compute the theoretical twist angle for the four prisms you tested and compare with your experimental results.
  4. What would the twist angle be for a snelson prism with infinitely many number of bars?

Test 2: Prism Stiffness

  1. Fill the tensegritoy box with one bag of marbles. This corresponds to a force of about 13.35 N. Record this force in the table.

  2. Set the 3-bar prism on the table. Place a sheet of paper under each node of the prism to allow for sliding. Measure the height of the prism. This is the rest height, record it in the table.

  3. Load the prism by placing the box on top. Measure the loaded height of the prism and record it in the table.

  4. Compute the displacement and use Hooke’s Law to compute the stiffness of the prism.

  5. If a bar has a mass of 5 g and a string has a mass of 0.5 g, compute the total mass of the prism and record it in the table.

  6. Repeat steps (a)-(e) for the 4, 5, and 6 bar prisms.

Discussion

  1. Which prism was the stiffest? the softest?
  2. One way to quantify the mass efficiency of the prism is to divide the stiffness of the prism by its mass. Using this measure, which prism is the most mass efficient? the least mass efficient?
  3. How do you think the stiffness of these prisms would compare to a solid block of wood of similar size? What about the mass efficiency?

Test 3: Tensegrity Plates

3-Bar Unit Plates

  1. Build two 3-bar snelson prisms of the same dimensions. Be sure to make the prisms as regular as possible.

  2. While keeping the bottoms of both prisms flat on the ground, find a way to connect the prisms together so that they are connected at two points (one on the top and one on the bottom).

  3. Using a similar attachment strategy, build and connect a third 3-bar snelson prism to the first two. This new prism should have two attachment points to the first prism and two attachment points to the second prism. The resulting plate should be quasi-triangular in shape.

  4. Using the connection method you discovered, expand upon your plate by building a circular plate. This plate should contain one unit in the middle, surrounded by a ring of six units, all connected in the same manner as before.

  5. Test the stiffness of this plate.

  6. Repeat this test, but this time see if you can discover a different way to connect the prisms to form the same basic plate shape.

n-Bar Unit Plates

  1. Build four 4-bar snelson prisms. Try to discover a method of connecting these four prisms together similar to the method used in Test 3 to make a square plate.

  2. Repeat this experiment for the 5-bar snelson prism.

  3. Repeat this experiment for the 6-bar snelson prism. You will only need three units this time to discover a plate topology.

Discussion

  1. For the two types of 3-Bar unit plates, which was the stiffest? Which was the most mass efficient?
  2. Were you able to discover ways to build flat plates with all the units (3, 4, 5, and 6 bar plates)?
  3. If you were able to build circular plate out of 6-bar units (similar to the one you built with 3-bar units) how would you expect the stiffness of this plate to compare to the stiffness of the 3-bar plate? the mass efficiency?
